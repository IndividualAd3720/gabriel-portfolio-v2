# From Doctor's Clinics to n8n Workflows: My Unlikely Career Pivot

**March 2026 · Automation**

---

Not many automation engineers started their careers memorizing the mechanism of action of celecoxib and pitching it to rheumatologists. I did.

For almost a year, I was a Professional Medical Representative at Getz Pharma — knocking on clinic doors across Metro Manila, managing a portfolio of 50+ healthcare professionals, conducting 10–15 visits a day. It was hard, relentless, ego-humbling work. And it made me a better engineer.

Here's what field sales taught me that four years of business analytics coursework didn't.

---

## 1. You Have 30 Seconds to Make the Point

Doctors are busy. When you walk into a clinic, you might have two minutes between patients. You learn to communicate with precision. One clear benefit. One supporting fact. One call to action.

I carry that instinct into every workflow I design. If an automation requires a 5-step explanation to justify, I haven't designed it well enough. The output should be obvious. The value should be immediate.

## 2. Relationships Are the System

No amount of product knowledge made doctors prescribe our medicines. Trust did. Consistency did. Showing up week after week, knowing their preferences, following up on what they mentioned last visit — that's what built the relationship.

Today, when I integrate with a client's Shopify or Lazada store, I'm not just writing API calls. I'm building a system that the client has to trust with their inventory, their customers, their revenue. The relationship model is the same — you earn it incrementally, you don't assume it.

## 3. Structured Days Beat Motivation

As a med rep, you had a route. You had a call plan. You hit it regardless of how you felt that morning. There was no "I'll do it when I'm inspired."

Automation work is the same. The workflows that actually ship are built by people who show up at the keyboard even when the problem is boring, even when the API documentation is terrible, even when the third attempt just failed with a 403 error.

## 4. Understanding the User Is Non-Negotiable

Before I could sell a pain management drug, I had to understand what pain the doctor was actually treating, what their patients looked like, what other drugs they were already prescribing. Selling celecoxib to a cardiologist required a completely different framing than selling it to an orthopedic surgeon.

Every automation project has the same requirement. You don't build what you think the client needs. You understand their actual workflow, their actual pain points, their actual team capacity — and you build for that.

---

## The Pivot

I left Getz Pharma in December 2024. Not because it was a bad job — it was a genuinely good one. But I knew my real interest was systems, automation, and building things that scaled beyond what a single person could do in a day.

I took a brief stint in technical support, got comfortable in CRM systems and troubleshooting under pressure, then landed a freelance automation engagement that became the work I do now.

The career path doesn't look linear. But looking back, each role handed me exactly what the next one needed.
